Tidying Data

library(tidyr)
library(tidyverse)
library(dplyr)

tidied_fertility <- fert_dat %>% pivot_longer(2:302, names_to = "year", 
    values_to = "fertility")
tidied_gdp <- GDP_dat %>% pivot_longer(2:61, names_to = "year", 
    values_to = "GDP_Per_Capita")
tidied_Co2 <- CO2_dat %>% pivot_longer(2:29, names_to = "year", 
    values_to = "CO2_emission")
tidied_life <- life_dat %>% pivot_longer(2:302, names_to = "year", 
    values_to = "life_expectancy")

After uploading and importing datasets that focus on various statistics for countries across the world, these datasets were then “tidied”. This was accomplished by using the pivot_longer function. The datasets originally displayed data for evry year as unique columns. This caused the datasets to be long and unwieldy. By using pivot_longer, year was made into its own variable and column to make the datasets more manageable.

Joining/Merging Data

gdp_life <- tidied_life %>% full_join(tidied_gdp) %>% na.omit()
Co2_gdp_life <- gdp_life %>% full_join(tidied_Co2) %>% na.omit()
full_tidied <- Co2_gdp_life %>% full_join(tidied_fertility) %>% 
    na.omit()
full_tidied <- full_tidied %>% separate(year, into = c(NA, "year"), 
    sep = 1, convert = TRUE)

Through using the full_join function, I was able to condense the 4 datsets into a single dataset that displayed the variables from every dataset. Full_join was specifically used because the datasets varied in the exact data they displayed. For example, one set might have data from 1800 to 2017, whereas another might have only the years 1990 to 2017. By using full_join, the rows of data that weren’t included in all four datasets were removed so that the final joined dataset would be more manageable. Additionally, na.omit was used because certain datasets were missing data for certain rows or columns. By removing these NA values, the dataset is more concise and easily examined. By joining in this manner, data from several datasets were dropped. The life expectancy and fertility datasets began in the year 1800 but after the join, all years up to 1990 were dropped. Additionally, many countries were dropped from several datasets because the CO2 emissions dataset included only 117 countries whereas others had closer to 180. These extra countries were dropped so that the CO2 dataset could be more effectively compared with the others. The cost of cutting out all this data is that the information that can be gained from examining the joined dataset is more limited than if all data had been preserved.

I started by looking at how certain facets of the data varied from years before 2000 to after 2000. I then also made a new variable, lifetime CO2 consumption, that is a measure of how much CO2 emission the average person in each country is responsible for over the course of their lifetime. This value was obtained by multiplying the life expectancy by CO2 emission value. Then 6 values, mean, standard deviation, variance, quantiles, maximum, and minimum were determined for all the variables. These same variables were then used when grouping by the country so that the values of the variables could be better understood on a country by country basis. One important thing to note from the data was an outlier in the life_expectancy values. In 1994, Rwanda had a life expectancy of approximately 9.64 years whereas the second and third lowest values were 44.3 and 44.4 years respectively. This outlier seemed likely to be some sort of mistake as a life expectancy of 9.64 is incredibly low. This value was later removed. On the other end of this variable, the highest life expectancies occurred in Singapore and Japan and sat at around 84 years of age. Average life expectancy was 70.9 years, average CO2 emission was 6.20 metric tons per person, average GDP per capita was 14,890 USD, and the average fertility was 2.83 children per woman.

This correlation matrix displays how the various variables correlate with each other. The one notable outlying variable from this heatmap is fertility. It has a negative correlation with all other variables whereas all other variables are positively correlated with each other. This makes sense goven that high fertility is often a sign of lesser developed countries and as countries develop their fertility tends to decrease. Every variable other than fertility is associated with advancement or higher quality of living, such as life expectancy and GDP. The year is also positively correlated with all variables except fertility. This suggests that, on average, as time moves forward, countries have increases in CO2 emission, life expectancy, GDP per capita, and lifetime CO2 consumption.
